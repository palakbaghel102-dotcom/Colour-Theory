Primary Colors
Red, Yellow, and Blue are the 3 primary colors. They cannot be made by mixing other colors. All other colors come from these three.
Secondary Colors
Mix two primaries:
● Red + Yellow = Orange
● Yellow + Blue = Green
● Red + Blue = Violet
Tertiary Colors
Mix a primary with its neighboring secondary. There are 6 — they sit between the others on the wheel.
Color Schemes
A color scheme is a planned set of colors that work well together. Here are the four core types every designer must know.
Scheme 01
Complementary
Two colors directly opposite on the wheel. Maximum contrast — each makes the other more vibrant. Perfect for CTAs in App UI.
High contrast · Bold · Energetic
Examples: Netflix, Firefox, Fanta
Scheme 02
Analogous
Three colors next to each other on the wheel. Harmonious and natural-looking — like a sunset. Low contrast but very pleasing.
Harmonious · Natural · Calm
Examples: Wellness apps, nature photography
Scheme 03
Triadic
Three colors equally spaced 120° apart. Very vibrant and playful. Use one dominant and two as accents to avoid visual chaos.
Vibrant · Playful · Balanced
Examples: Burger King, Toy Story, children's games
Scheme 04
Monochromatic
One hue in multiple shades and tints. Clean, elegant, and very easy to get right. Great for professional apps and portfolios.
Elegant · Minimal · Professional
Examples: Linear, Notion, most fintech apps
The RGB Color Model
Every color on a screen is made from three beams of light: Red, Green, and Blue. Drag the sliders to mix your own.

💡 Additive Mixing
Screens use light, so colors add up. All three at 255 = white. All at 0 = black. Opposite to mixing paint!
🔢 Values 0 to 255
Each channel has 256 values (0–255). Combined: 256³ = 16.7 million possible colors.
🎨 RGB vs HEX
HEX is just RGB in base-16. #1A6FDB = R:26, G:111, B:219. Same color, different format.
🖨️ RGB vs CMYK
RGB is for screens. Printers use CMYK (ink). Colors may look different when printed!

Contrast ratio
What is contrast ratio?
Contrast ratio measures how different two colors appear to the human eye. It ranges from 1:1 (identical) to 21:1 (pure black on white). Higher = more readable.
WCAG Guidelines
AA Standard — minimum for most text: 4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text (18px+ or bold 14px+).

AAA Standard — enhanced: 7:1 for normal text, 4.5:1 for large text. Required for critical accessibility.
Why does it matter?
About 1 in 12 men and 1 in 200 women have some form of color vision deficiency. Poor contrast also affects users in bright sunlight, on low-quality screens, and those with visual impairments.
Quick tips
• Dark text on light background usually works best
• Never use red text on green (or vice versa)
• Avoid light grey text on white backgrounds
• Always test your primary CTA button color combo

✅ Use icons alongside color
Never rely on color alone to communicate meaning. Add icons, labels, or patterns so colorblind users get the same information.
✅ Avoid red-green combinations
Red-green color blindness is the most common type. Avoid using red and green together to signal opposite states like success/error.
✅ Test with sufficient contrast
High contrast ratios (4.5:1+) help all users including those with color vision deficiency distinguish elements on screen.
✅ Use blue-orange instead of red-green
Blue-orange is a safe complementary pair that works well for all types of color vision deficiency — like our App UI palette!
✅ Add texture and patterns
In charts and graphs, use different textures or patterns to distinguish data categories rather than relying on color alone.
